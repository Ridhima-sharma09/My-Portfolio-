About me

Hi! I'm Ridhima Sharma, a 17-year-old student, developer, and poet from Delhi. I've always been unapologetically curious — from the logic tucked inside organic chemistry to the philosophy hidden between book pages.

I've spent four years preparing for JEE, and that journey reshaped how I think: structured, analytical, and driven to understand things deeply rather than just skim the surface. I'm currently giving my Class 12 boards as a consistent top scorer in Chemistry.

Alongside equations, I sketch realistic portraits (a hobby since literally my birth year), journal my thoughts in ink, and collect quotes that quietly rearrange how I see the world.

Anne Frank wrote "I want to go on living even after my death." I think about that often. I want every project, every line of code, every word I write to carry something real and lasting. 🌙

What sparked my interest
From pencil sketches
to lines of code 🎨
✏️
Age 7 — I started drawing realistic portraits. I'd sit for hours, studying how light falls on a cheekbone, how a shadow tells a story.
↓
🧪
Class 9 — Science Club & Cybersecurity Club opened the door. My first webinar on AI and software development left me electric with possibility.
↓
💻
First Python project — and I realised: code is just another kind of drawing. Different tools, same eye for detail and same desire to make something real.
↓
🌸
Today — building apps people actually use: a journal for stressed students, a tracker for pharmacies. The artist and the developer are the same person.

People often assume that moving from art to technology is a departure — like trading one world for another. For me, it never felt that way. It felt like an extension.

When I was drawing portraits, I was learning how to observe carefully, how to be patient with details, how to keep iterating until something felt true. When I started coding, I found those same instincts waiting for me there.

The sketch of a face and the structure of a Python function both demand the same thing: that you look closely, think deliberately, and care about what you're making.

In Class 9, attending my first cybersecurity and AI webinar through school's Science Club was the spark. I remember thinking — this is a language I want to learn. Not instead of drawing, but alongside it.

The Last Bookmark was born from that crossroads — a journaling app that thinks like a poet and works like a programmer. Soft on the outside, precise on the inside.

Coding never replaced my pencil. It just gave me a second one — one that could reach more people, solve real problems, and leave something behind that lasts.
